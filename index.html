    Research Assistant at the Higgins Psychology Lab

    • Timeline: June 2023 - August 2023
    • Mentor: Emma Miller

    As a research assistant at the Columbia Higgins Psychology Lab, I assisted Emma Miller (PhD Student in Psychology) using Natural Language Processing (NLP) and R to analyze conversational dynamics among newly acquainted strangers to prove the “shared reality” theory in conversation: the development of a shared interpretation about a thing being discussed. Our specific research question was: Does stronger sentiment intensity in conversation predict a stronger experience of shared reality with one’s conversation partner? We hypothesized that participants who exhibit greater sentiment intensity in their conversation messages will experience a stronger sense of shared reality with their partner.

    Covid Salud: Wellness App in Español

    • Live Project: Covid Salud
    • Timeline: Spetember 2020 - November 2020

    As the New England foliage flourished into beautiful colors in my hometown of Nashua in 2020, another COVID-related issue emerged in my community. Nashua, with the largest Latine population in New Hampshire, was disproportionately affected by COVID-19. I was anxious thinking about my friends from my school choir and their families. I melded this anxiety into a more useful form by reaching across to the Latinx community through employing the power of technology. This prompted me to devise a wellness app prototype providing the latest COVID-19 information from the CDC in Spanish, at the touch of a button. Relying on my conversational Spanish from school and experience teaching English to children in Costa Rica to create the content of this app turned out to be the easy part. I was confronted by the challenge of never having developed an app. Determined, I pivoted to the daunting process of teaching myself iOS App development languages: Xcode and Swift. While frustrating at times, I was filled with joy when my app prototype, “Covid Salud” became functional. In the future, I seek to understand why the Latinx population and other minority groups have health outcomes that are well below average, and why such groups are more susceptible to shocks like COVID-19.

    Covoogle: Forecasting and predicting future hotspots using Google Trends

    • Live Project: Covoogle
    • Timeline: June 2020 - August 2020

    Blaring reports of agony, maladies, and fatalities plagued our lives in 2020. As the pandemic unraveled, I was shocked as I witnessed the grave news reverberate unremittingly. COVID ravaged our communities with hotspots flaring and leaving public health officials struggling to combat those outbreaks due to a lag between COVID-19 and confirmed cases. I was unable to stay idle. I believed that I could harness the power of data to serve for the greater good, but I lacked the requisite background. I filled my secluded days of remote learning by teaching myself computational skills. As a lover of finding patterns in the obscure, I immersed myself in analyzing confirmed case data from the NY Times along with Google Search Trend data to predict hotspot states. After countless summer days and nights of agony and ecstasy playing with and charting the data deep within Google Trends, I identified a conclusive pattern. A spike in searches for “loss of taste” occurs two weeks before the official confirmed cases actually spike in the hotspot states of Texas, Florida, and Arizona. As I ecstatically tossed and turned to name the pattern I had discovered, the synergy that exists between COVID confirmed case data and Google Trend data, it finally hit me. ‘Covoogle’ adroitly described what I discovered. The process of unearthing a conclusive pattern amidst data mining and a spike in COVID cases ignited my passion for data and granted me the power to use science to serve all.
